Berms

How to Build a Berm

Berms are curved, banked turns designed to keep riders flowing through corners without braking hard.

  • 1. Choose the right location: Look for natural turns or create one where the trail would otherwise zigzag.
  • 2. Dig a base trench: About 30–50 cm deep, depending on the berm size. This creates a strong foundation.
  • 3. Stack layers: Use rocks, logs, and packed dirt. Start with the base layer (logs/rocks), then shovel clay/dirt on top.
  • 4. Shape the angle: Ideal berm angle is 45°. The exit should be slightly lower than the entry for smoother transition.
  • 5. Compact each layer: Use a tamper or stomp with your feet. Wetting the dirt slightly helps it bind better.
  • 6. Ride and refine: Test it with a few laps. Fix soft spots or ruts after some runs.

Drops

How to Build a Drop

Drops are sudden vertical transitions where the rider free-falls before landing on the trail again. They add thrill but require safety in design.

  • 1. Pick a location: Ideal spots are natural ledges, rock shelves, or elevated sections of trail.
  • 2. Build the takeoff: Use dirt or wood to create a stable platform. The edge should be solid — no crumbling dirt.
  • 3. Design the landing zone: Sloped and cleared of rocks/roots. Use packed dirt to absorb impact.
  • 4. Measure drop height: Start with 0.5–1 meter for beginners. Ensure there's enough run-up and room to land safely.
  • 5. Add rollable option (optional): For progression, create a side route that lets riders roll down instead of drop.
  • 6. Test with caution: Ride at medium speed, keep weight back, and compress slightly before takeoff.

Jumps

How to Build a Jump

Jumps are designed to send riders into the air and land them smoothly on a transition. They require careful shaping to avoid injury and maximize fun.

  • 1. Choose jump type:
    • Tabletop: Dirt-filled between lip and landing – good for beginners.
    • Double: Gap between lip and landing – requires commitment.
    • Step-up: Landing is higher than takeoff – easier on joints.
  • 2. Build the lip: Use packed dirt with a steep angle (35–60° depending on jump style). Shape it smooth and symmetric.
  • 3. Shape the landing: Sloped downhill landing, longer than the lip. It should mimic the arc of flight.
  • 4. Compact everything: The lip and landing must be rock-solid. Use water and compact in layers.
  • 5. Test with caution: Roll over it first, then slowly increase speed and pop.
  • 6. Add drainage: Place small channels around the sides to prevent erosion after rain.
